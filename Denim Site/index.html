Sustainable Denim

Problem: Impacts of Denim

Water Pollution

The three processes related to denim that cause water pollution are:

  1. the growing of cotton
  2. dyeing and finishing the material
  3. texturizing and finishing the product

Although growing cotton is a natural occurrence, the cotton-growing method for denim uses herbicides and chemical pesticides to yield the most cotton. The uses of these products contaminate water sources and soil; additionally, their applications can have adverse health effects on cotton farmers.

To dye denim, cotton is treated with several chemical dyes. The residual chemicals are washed off and dumped into water systems, turning them indigo-blue, as shown in the picture above of China's Pearl River. Farmers, who use water from sources near denim manufactures, have complained of sterile soils and chemically burned seedlings. Additionally, there are serious health risks for the workers involved in the dying process and the process of getting 'acid wash,' which consists in spraying denim with harmful acids.

Consumption of Water

Do you know how much water was used to make a pair of jeans!

Denim fabrics' main ingredient, cotton, is a fibre that is heavily watered and fertilized. Additionally, it uses large amounts of water in its manufacturing and packaging processes. The finishing processes employed, dyeing, washing and special visual effects (such as stone-washing) also consume vast quantities of water, resulting in denim manufacturing with a high-water footprint. Incredibly, one pair of jeans, including its production and general wear use up to 2,900 gallons of water.

Overall Supply Chain

The denim supply chain is unethical, from its employment of child labour, water pollution, unsustainable water consumption and the exposure of workers to harmful situations throughout the denim processing cycle.

To get cheap labour, many cotton farms, denim processing and manufacturing factories employ children. When working on processing denim, most workers are not provided with proper safety equipment. Furthermore, the entire process is unsustainable due to its water consumption, the method employed to grow cotton, and the heavy usage of harmful chemicals during the whole cycle.

Solution, Sustainable Denim!

Easy Solutions

Some easy/affordable options to make denim sustainable:

  • Upcycle: your old jeans could be broken down and reused as insulation in new home construction.
  • Rented: a Dutch company offers rental jeans for a down payment and a low monthly fee.
  • Recycled: Denim companies, such as Levi's, make denim that incorporates polyester fibers spun from discarded plastic bottles.
  • Organic: Many retailers now offer jeans made with organic cotton, grown without pesticides.
  • WashLess: Washing jeans after ten washes, saves 30L of water. Or spot washing instead.

Long Term Solutions

One long term solution is using better technologies throughout the denim creation cycle. An Italian company, Italdenim, is doing just that. They have invested in a machine that takes indigo dye and uses electro-chemicals (CO2 and O2) and no water to make the colours for their denim. This machine doesn't exist anywhere else in the world. Furthermore, the company applies chitosan (a natural product of chitin from the exoskeleton of shrimp and crab) to denim after dyeing it. This product protects dyes do they do not rub off, and it makes the denim more biodegradable. Also, this process uses less chemical, water and energy.

Another long term solution is changing techniques to be more sustainable. Artistic Milliners and DyStar, a Pakistan-based company, have done this by creating a new indigo dyeing technique called Crystal Clear dyeing. This process saves significant amounts of water, is salt-free, and uses 70% fewer chemicals. Moreover, DyStar uses 100% organic denim and clean energy to power their factories.

Jeanologia, a California based manufacturer, has developed several technologies to reduce water pollution and chemical consumption in denim manufacturing. One of these technologies includes lasers to give jeans a worn look instead of using sandblasting (the process involves air blasting sand at denim). Some other techniques are G2 ozone treatments, replaces the use of chemicals to fade the colour of jeans. And eflow technology that uses air (nanobubbles) instead of water to dye denim. Jeanologia works with many popular brands such as PVH Corp. (Calvin Klein, Tommy Hilfiger), Levi Strauss, V.F. Corp (makers of Lee and Wrangler)., H&M, Uniqlo and Marks & Spencer among others.
